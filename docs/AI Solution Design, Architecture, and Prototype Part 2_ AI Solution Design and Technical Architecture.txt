AI Solution Design, Architecture, and Prototype Part 2
AI Solution Design and Technical Architecture

Section 1. Problem Statement Executive Summary
This proposal addresses a specific and bounded business problem at Nike: inaccurate size and fit guidance in Nike’s digital channels, especially across footwear and apparel, which contributes to avoidable returns, lower conversion, weaker customer confidence, and margin leakage. The proposed solution is an AI-powered size and fit recommendation system that predicts the most likely correct size for a given customer and product, explains that recommendation in plain language, and improves over time through post-purchase feedback, return outcomes, and ongoing learning. Nike reported fiscal 2025 revenue of $46.3 billion, down 10 percent year over year, with NIKE Direct revenue of $18.8 billion, down 13 percent, and gross margin of 42.7 percent. In fiscal 2026 third quarter results, NIKE Direct was still down 4 percent and gross margin was 40.2 percent, showing that digital recovery and profitability restoration remain incomplete (Nike, Inc., 2025; Nike, Inc., 2026). Retail returns also remain economically significant. The National Retail Federation reported that retailers expect $849.9 billion in returned merchandise in 2025 and that 19.3 percent of online sales are expected to be returned, which is especially relevant for footwear and apparel where fit uncertainty is a major driver of returns (National Retail Federation, 2025; Kaushik et al., 2022). 

1.1 Specific Problem Statement
Nike currently lacks a sufficiently precise, scalable, and continuously learning size and fit decision layer across its digital commerce experience. Static size charts and broad category-level fit notes do not adequately capture the interaction between an individual customer’s purchase history, product-specific fit variation, style silhouette, return history, and stated fit preference. As a result, customers hesitate at the point of purchase, order multiple sizes, or return products because the fit is wrong. This reduces conversion, increases reverse-logistics cost, and weakens trust in Nike’s digital shopping experience.
This problem is appropriately bounded for an individual Part 2 submission. It focuses on one high-value decision point, the digital size-selection moment, rather than attempting enterprise-wide transformation. It is also well suited to a combination of AI and ML techniques, including supervised machine learning, ranking models, rules-based controls, and a limited generative AI layer for explanation.

1.2 Current-State Limitation and Context
Nike is better positioned than many competitors to solve this problem because it already has strong consumer-facing digital assets, valuable first-party customer data, and an established digital ecosystem. Nike has also previously invested in fit-related technology through acquisitions such as Invertex, which supported the development of Nike Fit, and Zodiac, which strengthened its customer analytics capabilities. This means the proposed initiative is not a speculative leap into an entirely new capability area. Instead, it extends an existing strategic direction toward more data-driven consumer decision support (Broukhim, P., 2020; The Times of Israel, 2018). Nike's most visible attempt to industrialize fit technology was Nike Fit, launched in May 2019. Built on the Invertex acquisition, Nike Fit used smartphone computer vision to scan a customer's foot, capture 13 morphological data points, and store a size profile within the NikePlus account. At launch, Nike cited internal data suggesting that 60 percent of consumers wear the wrong shoe size and that fit uncertainty was a leading driver of digital returns (Forbes, 2019; Bloomberg, 2019). However, Nike Fit was a static measurement tool rather than a continuously learning recommendation system. It captured a one-time foot scan but did not learn from purchase outcomes, return reasons, product-level fit variation, or qualitative signals across the catalog. By 2021 the feature had quietly failed to reach broad deployment across geographies and product lines, and it was never industrialized into a real-time decision layer at the point of digital purchase. That gap is precisely what this proposal addresses. Nike Fit demonstrated that the company recognized the fit problem early and had the technical ambition to pursue it. Nike Fit Intelligence is the initiative Nike Fit should have evolved into: a continuously learning, real-time recommendation layer that improves with every transaction rather than storing a static snapshot of a single measurement session. Despite that early investment, Nike still has a meaningful opportunity to industrialize fit intelligence more broadly across digital commerce touchpoints, especially at the exact moment when customers choose size and decide whether to purchase.

1.3 Measurable Success Definition
Success for this initiative should be measured at both business and technical levels. Within 12 to 18 months of scaled deployment, Nike should target a 15 percent relative reduction in fit-related digital return rate for in-scope categories, a 0.8 percentage point increase in digital conversion rate on sessions exposed to the recommendation service, and a 2 percentage point increase in repeat purchase rate among recommendation users. On the technical side, the initiative should target a top-1 recommendation accuracy of at least 70 percent on labeled historical data, confidence calibration error below 5 percent, real-time inference latency below 150 milliseconds, and no material fairness gap greater than 5 percentage points across monitored customer segments. These metrics are appropriate because they connect the AI system directly to financial performance, customer trust, and responsible deployment.

Section 2. AI Pipeline Solution Architecture and Design
2.1 Purpose, Scope, and Intended Business Outcomes
The target solution is a real-time AI service that recommends the best size for a customer-product pair on Nike.com and in Nike mobile apps. The intended business outcomes are higher conversion, fewer fit-related returns, stronger digital trust, lower reverse-logistics cost, and cleaner downstream demand signals for future merchandising and planning. The solution scope is intentionally narrow: it focuses on the size-selection decision in digital commerce, then expands later into richer feedback loops for product, merchandising, and membership experiences. 

2.2 Architecture Principles and Standards
The target architecture follows five core principles.
First, it is cloud-first. BigQuery is an appropriate data foundation because Google documents it as a fully managed, AI-ready data platform with a serverless architecture, built-in machine learning support, streaming ingestion, and governance capabilities. Vertex AI is appropriate because Google documents it as a unified platform for building, deploying, and scaling ML and generative AI systems, with end-to-end MLOps tools including pipelines, model registry, feature serving, experiments, and monitoring (Google Cloud, n.d.-a; Google Cloud, n.d.-b).
Second, it follows zero-trust security principles. All service calls, model endpoints, and data-access requests should use explicit authentication, authorization, least-privilege access, and continuous verification. This is especially important because the solution uses sensitive behavioral data and potentially measurement-derived inputs.
Third, it is responsible by design. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework is intended to help organizations manage risks and promote trustworthy and responsible development and use of AI systems. That makes it a suitable governance anchor for model cards, confidence thresholds, fairness checks, rollback controls, and human review for edge cases (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023).
Fourth, it aligns with recognized security and compliance frameworks. ISO/IEC 27001 provides an appropriate benchmark for information security management, risk governance, and control discipline, while FedRAMP offers a useful reference point for standardized cloud security assessment and assurance, even if Nike is not a federal agency (International Organization for Standardization, 2022; FedRAMP, n.d.).
Fifth, it is prototype realistic. The architecture uses common cloud-native components rather than requiring an impossible enterprise redesign. That matters because the later prototype must be feasible, not just conceptually attractive.

2.3 Target Architecture
The target architecture for Nike Fit Intelligence is designed as a cloud-first, API-driven decision system that integrates customer interaction, real-time inference, governed data pipelines, and continuous model monitoring within a production-ready AI service. This is appropriate for the problem because size recommendation requires low-latency inference at the product-detail-page level, continuous feedback from post-purchase outcomes, and careful control of model drift, fairness, and deployment risk. Cloud-native data and ML services are well suited to this design because they support large-scale data processing, managed model deployment, and repeatable MLOps workflows (Google Cloud, n.d.-a; Google Cloud, n.d.-b; Google Cloud, n.d.-c; Google Cloud, n.d.-d).
At a high level, the architecture has four layers. The first is the customer interaction layer, where Nike.com, the Nike App, and related digital channels present the size recommendation at the product detail page and capture interaction events. The second is the application and integration layer, where a Fit Decision API orchestrates the recommendation flow and integrates with commerce services. The third is the data and AI layer, where customer data, transaction data, return data, product data, and text feedback are processed into features and used for training and inference. The fourth is the governance and monitoring layer, where logging, drift detection, fairness checks, and deployment approval are managed.

2.3.1 Existing Environment, Strengths, and Limitations
Nike’s existing environment provides a strong foundation for this initiative because the company already operates at large digital scale, has a substantial direct-to-consumer business, and has previously invested in fit-related and customer-analytics capabilities through acquisitions such as Invertex and Zodiac (Broukhim, P., 2020; The Times of Israel, 2018). The primary strength of the existing environment is therefore data scale and digital reach. Nike already has the customer traffic, product breadth, and transaction history needed to make a recommendation engine commercially valuable.
At the same time, the current environment has clear limitations. Static size charts, broad fit notes, and non-personalized guidance do not adequately use customer history, product-level fit variation, or post-purchase outcomes at the moment of decision. This creates a gap between Nike’s digital scale and the precision of its fit guidance. The proposed system addresses that gap by turning size selection into a learning decision layer rather than a static lookup experience.

2.4 Written Explanation of the Data Pipeline, AI Techniques, and Technology Stack
The system begins when a shopper reaches a product detail page and interacts with the size selector. At that point, the storefront calls a Fit Decision API. That API retrieves relevant customer features, product features, and session context, then passes them to a production model endpoint. The final response contains a recommended size, a confidence score, an optional alternative size, and a plain-language explanation. This is a suitable real-time design because Cloud Run supports scalable application hosting, Vertex AI supports online prediction endpoints, and Vertex AI’s API surface supports production integration through managed prediction and feature services. 
The offline learning loop is equally important. Customer events, purchases, returns, return reasons, reviews, and product master data should be ingested into a governed data pipeline and stored in BigQuery. BigQuery’s documentation highlights streaming ingestion, serverless analytics, SQL and Python interfaces, built-in ML capabilities, and governance features, which makes it a strong fit for historical data management, feature engineering, and training dataset assembly. For ETL orchestration and validation, the pipeline should use Koheesio, an open-source data engineering framework built on Apache Spark, which supports scalable identity
resolution, feature generation, and label creation workflows within the BigQuery
ecosystem (Koheesio, n.d.). Vertex AI then supports the rest of the ML lifecycle, including experiments, training, pipelines, model registry, feature serving, explainability, and model monitoring.
The AI stack should be hybrid. The core recommendation engine should use supervised machine learning, especially tabular models such as gradient boosted trees or ranking models, because the task is structured around customer attributes, product attributes, and historical outcomes. A second layer can use embeddings from product descriptions or review text to capture qualitative fit signals such as “runs narrow” or “oversized.” A third layer should remain rules-based to enforce safety constraints, such as inventory availability, regional size conversion, and minimum confidence thresholds. A fourth layer can use generative AI only for explanation, not for the actual recommendation decision, which keeps the system auditable and lowers hallucination risk. This division of labor is consistent with the responsible AI and MLOps controls described by NIST and Google Cloud. 

2.4.1 Integration and Interfaces
The integration strategy is API-based and modular. Nike.com and Nike mobile applications should call a Fit Decision API at the product-detail-page level when a shopper opens the size selector or views a product. The API should exchange structured JSON payloads containing customer context, product attributes, session context, recommendation output, confidence score, and explanation text. This modular design improves interoperability because the recommendation service can evolve independently of the commerce front end while still integrating cleanly with digital product pages, mobile apps, analytics pipelines, and monitoring services.

2.5 Data Design
The solution uses six data source families: customer profile data, behavioral event data, product master data, transaction outcome data, text data such as reviews and customer-service notes, and optional measurement-derived data for users who explicitly opt in. The data environment must handle structured, semi-structured, and limited text data. Velocity is mixed. Clickstream and product-page interactions should flow in near real time, while retraining and some return labels can be handled in batch or micro-batch windows.
Data preparation includes identity resolution, event deduplication, missing-value handling, regional size normalization, label creation for fit-related returns, and denoising where return reasons are ambiguous. This data design supports the selected AI techniques because size recommendation depends on the interaction of customer history, product structure, and observed post-purchase outcomes. Nike's NikePlus membership program has exceeded 160 million members globally, providing a large base of labeled transactions and return history for supervised training (Nike, Inc., n.d.). At peak digital traffic, the system should be designed to handle tens of thousands of concurrent product-detail-page sessions, meaning the feature-serving layer must support sub-50-millisecond feature retrieval and the inference endpoint must sustain at least several hundred real-time prediction requests per second without degradation.

2.5.1 Feature Engineering and Representation Strategy
The feature store for Nike Fit Intelligence should organize inputs into three families. Customer features include historical size selections by category, past return rate, fit preference signals, stated fit preference (if captured), and geographic sizing region. Product features include category, silhouette type, construction material, size curve distribution, historical return rate by size, and any available measurement specifications. Session features include device type, session recency, and whether the customer is new or returning. For the embedding layer, product descriptions and review text should be encoded using a lightweight sentence-transformer model to produce dense vector representations that capture qualitative fit signals such as "runs narrow" or "true to size." These embeddings allow the system to generalize fit signals across products with similar text descriptions, even when transaction history is sparse, directly addressing the cold-start problem for new SKUs.

2.6 AI Techniques
The proposed system is hybrid. The primary decision engine should use supervised machine learning on structured data. A strong baseline is a gradient boosted tree classifier or ranker trained on customer, product, and session features. A second layer can use embedding-based representation from product descriptions and review text to capture qualitative fit signals and improve cold-start performance for new products. A third layer is rules-based, overriding model output when inventory is unavailable, confidence is too low, or regional conversion requirements apply. A fourth, limited layer uses generative AI for explanation only. Generative AI should not determine the recommendation itself. Instead, it should translate structured outputs into customer-friendly language. This preserves auditability and supports responsible AI design. Model development and experimentation should use Python-based tooling such as XGBoost or LightGBM for tabular learning, scikit-learn for preprocessing and baseline evaluation, and Vertex AI Experiments for run tracking and model comparison.

2.6.1 Evaluation and Validation Approach
Model evaluation should occur in two stages. Offline evaluation uses a holdout set of historical labeled transactions to assess top-1 accuracy, top-2 accuracy, precision and recall by size band, confidence calibration error, and segment-level fairness gaps across size ranges and geographic regions. A model that does not meet the accuracy and fairness thresholds defined in Section 1.3 should not advance to production. Online evaluation uses a structured A/B test during the Phase 3 pilot, randomly assigning product-detail-page sessions to a treatment group receiving the AI recommendation and a control group receiving the current static size chart. Primary metrics are fit-related return rate, conversion rate, and recommendation adoption rate. Statistical significance thresholds and minimum detectable effect sizes should be defined before the pilot launches to prevent premature scale-up decisions.

2.7 Security, Compliance, and Responsible AI
Because fit guidance may involve sensitive behavioral information and potentially measurement-related inputs, security and governance are central to the design. The service should use authenticated service-to-service communication, least-privilege access, encryption in transit and at rest, versioned deployment, rollback controls, and audit logging. Responsible AI controls should include model cards, intended-use documentation, prohibited-use definitions, segment-level fairness testing, confidence thresholds, and human review for edge cases. The National Institute of Standards and Technology’s AI Risk Management Framework is an appropriate governance anchor because it is specifically intended to help organizations manage AI risk and promote trustworthy AI across design, development, deployment, and use (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023). In practice, that means the recommendation service should include model cards, intended-use documentation, prohibited-use definitions, segment-level fairness testing, confidence thresholds, rollback controls, and human review for edge cases. These controls should be paired with zero-trust access design, audit logging, and disciplined change management so that the system is governed as a responsible AI service rather than only a prediction model.

2.8 Deployment, Operations, and Monitoring
The recommended hosting model is cloud-native and hybrid-integrated. Core AI services should run in Google Cloud, with BigQuery as the governed data layer, Vertex AI for model training, registry, monitoring, and online inference, and Cloud Run hosting the Fit Decision API that integrates with Nike’s digital commerce stack through APIs. Inference should be real-time for product-detail-page usage, while retraining should occur on a regular schedule and be adjusted based on drift signals and data velocity. Operational monitoring should include API latency, recommendation adoption rate, model confidence distribution, prediction drift, training-serving skew, and fairness gaps across monitored segments. Google Cloud’s Vertex AI documentation describes model monitoring, pipelines, feature management, model registry, and online inference services as part of a repeatable MLOps workflow, which makes this deployment approach practical rather than theoretical (Google Cloud, n.d.-b; Google Cloud, n.d.-d). To strengthen operational resilience, the service should also use multi-region data backups, infrastructure-as-code recovery playbooks, and defined recovery time and recovery point objectives for the recommendation API and feature-serving layer.

2.9 Roadmap and Implementation Plan
Phase 1, months 0 to 3. Define in-scope categories, build the fit-related return label taxonomy, clean return-reason data, establish the data contract, and create the initial historical training dataset.
Phase 2, months 4 to 6. Train baseline tabular models, build the recommendation API, create rule-based safeguards, and complete offline evaluation for accuracy, calibration, and fairness.
Phase 3, months 7 to 9. Launch a pilot in one footwear segment and one apparel segment using A/B testing on digital product pages.
Phase 4, months 10 to 15. Scale to additional categories, integrate review-text signals, operationalize monitoring, and automate retraining workflows.
Phase 5, months 16 to 24. Extend the system into richer member fit profiles, product-creation feedback loops, and future made-for-you assortment opportunities.

2.10 Risks, Trade-Offs, and Mitigation
The first risk is data quality. Return-reason data is often noisy and not always explicitly labeled as fit-related. The mitigation is a stronger labeling framework, weak supervision, and human review of ambiguous cases.
The second risk is fairness. The model may perform better for common size bands than fringe sizes or differently across geographies. The mitigation is segment-level monitoring, fairness thresholds, and policy-based fallbacks.
The third risk is privacy. Some customers may perceive fit intelligence as intrusive, especially if measurement-derived inputs are used. The mitigation is explicit consent, data minimization, and clear user-facing disclosures.
The fourth risk is cold start. New products and new customers reduce predictive precision. The mitigation is product embeddings, category-level priors, and conservative defaults.
The fifth risk is over-automation. If the system is wrong with high confidence, customer trust can erode quickly. The mitigation is confidence gating, explanation, and visible user choice rather than forced automation.

Section 3. Business Case
3.1 Strategic Fit
This solution aligns directly with Nike’s strategy because it improves digital conversion quality rather than simply adding generic personalization. Nike describes itself as a growth company that creates innovative products, builds deep personal connections with consumers, and delivers an integrated marketplace with compelling retail experiences (Nike, Inc., n.d.). A size and fit recommendation engine supports all three by improving the purchase decision at the point where customer confidence, conversion, and margin all intersect. It is also differentiating because a fit engine improves with scale, scope, and learning. The more customers interact with it, the more accurate it becomes. That creates a defensible data flywheel that smaller competitors will struggle to replicate at a comparable scale. 
In the competitive landscape, Nike faces differentiated threats from multiple directions. Amazon Fashion and Zalando have invested heavily in size recommendation and virtual try-on features at scale (Zalando SE, 2025), while Stitch Fix has built its entire business model around personalized fit intelligence. Direct competitors such as Adidas have also expanded their digital direct-to-consumer capabilities. However, Nike's advantage lies in the combination of first-party transaction depth, global NikePlus membership data, and control of the full digital purchase and return loop without marketplace intermediaries. A competitor using a third-party size recommendation tool cannot close the feedback loop between recommendation, purchase, return reason, and model retraining the way Nike can through its owned digital ecosystem. That closed loop is the structural moat. It means Nike's model compounds in accuracy over time in a way that marketplace-dependent competitors cannot replicate.

3.2 Sustainable Competitive Advantage and Flywheel
The solution creates advantage through scale, scope, and learning. Scale comes from Nike’s large digital traffic and direct business. Scope comes from using one fit-intelligence layer across footwear, apparel, and future categories. Learning comes from the fact that every accepted recommendation, kept purchase, exchange, and return improves future model performance. This creates a flywheel: better fit recommendations increase confidence and conversion, which generates more purchases and more labeled data, which improves model quality, which reduces returns and raises margin, which funds broader deployment and stronger customer experience. 

3.3 New Value Chain and Business Model Impact
The value chain changes in three ways. First, the product detail page becomes an AI-guided decision point. Second, returns data becomes a strategic learning asset rather than only an operational cost. Third, product creation and merchandising gain feedback on where fit issues are concentrated by silhouette, size curve, or construction pattern.
Over time, this capability can support new business models such as richer member fit profiles, improved made-to-order personalization, and stronger pre-launch fit validation for new products.
The operating model also changes in meaningful ways. The merchandising team gains a new feedback signal, fit return rates by silhouette and size curve, that can inform product construction decisions before launch rather than after. The digital product team shifts from managing static size content to owning a live AI service with defined SLAs, monitoring dashboards, and retraining cadences. A new cross-functional working group spanning data science, digital product, and responsible AI governance becomes a standing operational requirement rather than a one-time project team. These operating model changes are necessary investments, but they also signal that fit intelligence is becoming a core capability rather than a feature, which increases its long-term defensibility.

3.4 Business Outcomes and Financial Analysis
The business case for Nike Fit Intelligence is strong because it improves both revenue quality and cost structure at the same time. Unlike broader AI-transformation programs, this initiative targets one commercially sensitive decision point in digital commerce where even modest improvement can create measurable value. That is especially relevant given Nike’s recent performance. Nike reported fiscal 2025 revenue of $46.3 billion, NIKE Direct revenue of $18.8 billion, and gross margin of 42.7 percent, while fiscal 2026 third quarter results still showed NIKE Direct down 4 percent and gross margin at 40.2 percent, indicating that digital productivity and margin recovery remain incomplete (Nike, Inc., 2025; Nike, Inc., 2026). At the same time, the broader economics of returns remain severe. The National Retail Federation reported that retailers are expected to see $849.9 billion in returned merchandise in 2025, and 19.3 percent of online sales are expected to be returned, which is especially relevant for digital apparel and footwear businesses where size and fit uncertainty is a major driver of avoidable returns (National Retail Federation, 2025; Kaushik et al., 2022).
For the base case, the financial model assumes that phase one covers only a subset of Nike’s digital business rather than the whole enterprise. Specifically, it assumes the initiative is first deployed across categories representing 25 percent of NIKE Direct revenue. Using Nike’s fiscal 2025 NIKE Direct revenue of $18.8 billion, that produces an in-scope revenue base of $4.7 billion (Nike, Inc., 2025). To remain conservative, the model assumes a baseline fit-related return rate of 19 percent in scope, broadly aligned with NRF’s estimate that 19.3 percent of online sales will be returned in 2025 (National Retail Federation, 2025; Kaushik et al., 2022). It then assumes that Nike Fit Intelligence reduces fit-related returns in those categories by 3 percentage points, from 19 percent to 16 percent, and also improves conversion enough to produce a 1.0 percent revenue uplift within the in-scope categories. These are credible assumptions for a focused AI intervention because they do not require dramatic enterprise-wide behavior change.
Under this base case, avoided returned sales value equals approximately $141 million, calculated as $4.7 billion multiplied by the 3 percentage point reduction in return rate. To avoid overstating impact, the model does not treat all avoided returns as fully incremental profit. Instead, it values the benefit through three channels. First, retained gross profit from sales that are no longer lost through avoidable fit-related returns. Using Nike’s 42.7 percent gross margin benchmark from fiscal 2025, retained gross profit on $141 million of avoided returns equals approximately $60.2 million (Nike, Inc., 2025). Second, reverse-logistics and handling savings. Assuming avoided reverse-logistics cost equals 10 percent of avoided returned sales value, Nike gains an additional $14.1 million. Third, conversion uplift. A 1.0 percent increase in in-scope digital revenue adds approximately $47 million in incremental sales. Applying the same 42.7 percent gross margin yields about $20.1 million in additional gross profit. Together, these components produce an annual gross benefit of about $94.4 million before ongoing operating expense.
That base-case annual benefit can be summarized simply. Avoided fit-related returns generate about $60.2 million in retained gross profit. Lower reverse-logistics and handling costs generate another $14.1 million. Incremental sales from higher conversion generate about $20.1 million in additional gross profit. Combined, the initiative produces approximately $94.4 million in annual gross economic benefit in the first scaled year.
The cost profile is also realistic for an enterprise AI capability. A credible estimate is $28 million in Year 0 implementation cost, followed by $12 million in Year 1 run cost, $13 million in Year 2, and $14 million in Year 3. Under those assumptions, the initiative remains attractive even with conservative ramp-up. In the conservative case, annual gross benefits are roughly $63 million once scaled. In the base case, annual gross benefits are approximately $94.4 million. In the upside case, where return reduction reaches 4 percentage points and revenue uplift reaches 1.5 percent, annual gross benefits are approximately $129.2 million.
The $28 million Year 0 cost is broken down by component in the accompanying Excel model, covering data engineering and BigQuery setup ($5M), Koheesio ETL pipeline build ($3M), Vertex AI model development ($6M), Fit Decision API build on Cloud Run ($4M), Nike.com and Nike App integration ($5M), governance and responsible AI controls ($2M), and program management ($3M).
The business case also depends on the AI system improving over time as labeled data accumulates. At Phase 3 pilot launch, the model is expected to achieve the baseline accuracy targets defined in Section 1.3, with a top-1 recommendation accuracy of at least 70 percent on historical labeled data. As the system processes 12 to 18 months of live recommendation, purchase, and return data from in-scope categories, accuracy should improve toward a 75 to 80 percent top-1 accuracy range based on the volume of new labeled examples generated. Each percentage point improvement in top-1 accuracy is expected to translate into a further reduction in fit-related return rate, creating a compounding economic benefit that is not fully captured in the conservative base-case estimate. This learning dynamic is what makes the flywheel financially meaningful beyond the first year.
Using the base-case assumptions in this document, Year 1 net benefit is approximately $82.4 million, Year 2 net benefit is approximately $81.4 million, and Year 3 net benefit is approximately $80.4 million after run costs. At a 12 percent discount rate, the three-year NPV is approximately $167.6 million. Using average annual net benefit divided by initial implementation cost as a simple annualized ROI measure, the initiative generates an annualized ROI of approximately 181.6 percent. These figures materially exceed the return threshold that would normally justify a targeted AI initiative of this scale.
The key point is that Nike does not need this model to transform the entire enterprise for the economics to work. It only needs a modest reduction in avoidable fit-related returns and a modest increase in conversion in a limited slice of digital revenue. Because Nike’s digital scale is already large, even a focused improvement creates disproportionate financial value. That makes this initiative financially stronger than a vague personalization investment and more feasible than a full enterprise-wide AI transformation.

3.5 Risk-Adjusted Scenario Assessment
The financial case remains attractive even after applying execution risk. The key uncertainty is not whether size and fit guidance matters economically. The evidence strongly suggests that it does. The real uncertainty is whether Nike can execute the model, data, user-experience, and governance components well enough to capture that value in production. For that reason, Nike should treat the initiative as a staged investment with three gates. The first gate is offline model quality, including accuracy, calibration, and fairness thresholds. The second gate is live pilot evidence on return reduction and conversion uplift. The third gate is operational reliability, including latency, drift monitoring, and adoption. If the initiative misses two of those three gates, scale-up should pause until the design is corrected.

3.6 Final Recommendation
Nike should move forward with this initiative because it is strategically aligned, technically feasible, financially credible, and appropriately bounded. It addresses a real customer and margin problem at the point where Nike already has relative strength: the digital consumer interface. It also creates a learning system that compounds over time through scale, scope, and feedback. Just as importantly, it is prototype-friendly. A later prototype can be built with historical Nike orders, return reasons, product attributes, and a simplified recommendation model using BigQuery for data storage and feature preparation, Vertex AI for model training and deployment, and a Cloud Run-hosted Fit Decision API for decision delivery into Nike.com and the Nike App.
